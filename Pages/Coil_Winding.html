Coil Winding Machine

This is a quick and simple machine to wind a coil.
I made this machine with available parts in my lab.


Background Story

When I was studying for my bachelor's degree, I got interested in making a taser gun for fun. I watched so many YouTube videos to get some ideas and make one. The majority of the videos that I watched, used off-the-shelf high-voltage transformers to generate voltages above 100kV. These transformers are quite cheap and accessible through eBay and Amazon however, due to sanctions against the country, banking, and shipping restrictions, I decided to make a transformer in my lab.
I found some transformers with ferrite cores in my lab and removed all the existing windings. I calculated the primary and secondary voltage ratio of the transformer and using some online tools, found a suitable wire for this purpose. Because the wire was so thin (probably around 0.2mm), the winding process was so difficult.
To generate 50 to 100kV in the secondary of the transformer, I needed to wind the coil for a couple of thousand turns. As I remember, I did the winding manually almost 20 times but failed to finish it because of difficulty in counting turns and breaking the wire accidentally. Eventually, I decided to make the coil winding machine for this purpose.

Key Features

This machine has a 12v DC motor with a simple PWM speed controller. Using a NE555 timer I managed to make a PWM signal and adjust the speed of the motor using a potentiometer (volume). To count the turns, I used a hall sensor and a magnet attached to the coil holder shaft so I could get a signal in every revolution then fed these signals to an Arduino to increment a counter. The Arduino converts these values to arrays that could drive a 3-digit seven-segment and show the turns accurately.

Challenges and Solutions

The high speed of the DC motor was one of my concerns in this project but I had a motor with a gearbox attached to it to reduce the speed and enhance the torque. Other than that, I used my benchtop power supply to power up all the control circuits as well as the DC motor.
Because I needed to wind the coil roughly between 1000 and 2000 turns, I used the decimal point to indicate that the number was over 1000.
The major challenge was making the mechanical fixture and assemble all together. I didn't have a 3D printer and I had to make something from scratch to hold everything together. I used a whiteboard marker as a shaft to hold the core of the coil and glued the magnet to it. I also glued the shaft of the motor to the marker because didn't have a shaft coupling.

Final Words

I tried to make this machine as quick as possible so I could get back to making a DIY taser. Finally, I managed to wind a coil for my project however, I couldn't make a reliable taser and had some component failures. I learned a lot about driving 7-segments, Arduino counter, hall sensor, DC motor, and NE555 motor driver. This project could be further developed with the possibility of 3D-printing the mechanical parts. Other than that, a wire traveller could also make this machine fully autonomous.
